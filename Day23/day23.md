## 5. Key Learnings

- **Customer clarity and PMF potential are not the same score, and they shouldn't be.** Knowing exactly who my customer is (78/100) doesn't mean I've proven the product actually works for them (58/100) — the blueprint forced a clean separation between "I understand the customer" and "I've proven the solution."
- **"What NOT to build" was more useful than "what to build."** Explicitly ruling out the full knowledge base, mobile app, and enterprise features stopped scope creep before writing a line of code — the MVP is now a single, testable workflow instead of a mini-platform.
- **MoSCoW turned a vague roadmap into a defensible v1 scope.** Separating Must/Should/Could/Won't made it obvious that staleness-flagging is core (Must) while multi-team support is a luxury (Could) — a distinction that was blurry before.
- **Pricing needs to be tested, not assumed.** The report explicitly flagged that the $299–$499/month hypothesis is untested and told me to validate it directly in interviews rather than baking it into the plan as fact.
- **The verdict system (🟢🟡🟠🔴) makes it harder to fool yourself.** A flat "Conditional Go" from Day 22 became a more precise "Promising but Unvalidated" here — useful because it's honest about *where* the gap is (solution proof) rather than implying the whole idea is shaky.
