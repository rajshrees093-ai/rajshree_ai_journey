MODULE 1 · FOUNDATIONS

Understanding Correlation

Before we draw a single line through data, we need a way to measure whether two variables even have a relationship worth modeling.

What correlation actually measures

Correlation describes the strength and direction of a straight-line relationship between two numeric variables. When one variable rises, does the other tend to rise too (positive), fall (negative), or do the two have no consistent pattern at all (zero/near-zero)?

The standard tool for this is the Pearson correlation coefficient, written r. It always lands between -1 and +1:

r = Σ[(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)] / √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² · Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²]

Don't worry about computing this by hand every time — what matters is reading the result. r = 1 is a perfect uphill line, r = -1 is a perfect downhill line, and r = 0 means no linear pattern at all.

Analogy Think of correlation like two dancers on a stage. When r is close to +1, they move in lockstep — one steps forward, the other steps forward too, every time. When r is close to -1, they're mirror partners — one steps forward exactly as the other steps back. When r is near 0, they're improvising independently: watching one dancer tells you nothing about what the other will do next.

The four shapes of a scatter plot

Same "zero correlation" reading can hide very different realities — always look at the scatter plot, not just the number.
Worked example Ice cream sales and drowning incidents both rise in summer. If you compute r for these two variables across the year, you'll likely get a strong positive value, around r ≈ 0.7. That doesn't mean ice cream causes drowning — both are driven by a third variable: hot weather, which pushes people toward both ice cream stands and swimming pools.

Correlation vs. causation

CorrelationCausation
Two variables move together in a measurable, consistent wayChanging one variable directly produces a change in the other
Can be checked with a single formula (r) on observational dataUsually requires controlled experiments or careful causal-inference design
Vulnerable to confounding variables (a hidden third cause)Accounts for and rules out confounders by design
Symmetric: r(x,y) = r(y,x)Directional: X causes Y is not the same claim as Y causes X

Common misconceptions
  • "Correlation proves causation." It never does on its own — see the ice cream example above.
  • "r = 0 means no relationship." It means no linear relationship. The curved example above has a strong pattern with r near zero.
  • "A higher r is always better." Context matters — in social sciences r = 0.3 can be meaningful, while in physics r = 0.3 might signal a broken instrument.
Key takeaways
  • Correlation (r) measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship, ranging from -1 to +1.
  • Always eyeball the scatter plot — the number alone can hide curves, clusters, and outliers (Anscombe's Quartet is the classic proof of this, coming up in Module 4).
  • Correlation is a necessary but never sufficient condition for causation.

MODULE 2 · APPLICATION

Simple Linear Regression

Correlation tells you two variables move together. Regression goes further: it gives you an equation you can actually use to predict one from the other.

Fitting a line through the noise

A simple linear regression finds the straight line that best summarizes the relationship between a predictor x and an outcome y:

ŷ = b₀ + b₁x   (intercept b₀, slope b₁, predicted value ŷ)

b₁ (the slope) tells you how much ŷ changes for every one-unit increase in x. b₀ (the intercept) is the predicted value when x = 0. The line is chosen using the least squares method: out of every possible line, pick the one that minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances between the actual points and the line.

Minimize: Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²  →  this vertical gap is called a residual
Analogy Imagine a crowd of people walking roughly northeast across a field, each drifting a little off-course. The regression line is the single straight path a tour guide would draw to best represent where the group as a whole is heading — not matching anyone exactly, but minimizing how far off it is from everyone, with big deviations penalized more heavily than small ones (because we square the residuals).

Seeing residuals

The regression line minimizes the sum of the squares of every dashed residual segment simultaneously.

R²: how much variance does the line explain?

R² = 1 − (Σ residual² / Σ total deviation from mean²)

R² ranges from 0 to 1 and tells you the proportion of variation in y that's explained by x. An R² of 0.81 means 81% of the spread in exam scores can be explained by hours studied — the remaining 19% comes from other factors (sleep, prior knowledge, luck) not in the model.

Common misconceptions
  • "I can extrapolate confidently far beyond my data." A line fit on 0–10 hours of study shouldn't be trusted to predict scores at 40 hours — the relationship may not stay linear.
  • "Regression toward the mean is a modeling trick." It's a real statistical phenomenon: extreme observations tend to be followed by more average ones, purely from randomness, not causation.
  • "A steep slope means a strong relationship." Slope depends on units; strength is better read from r or R², not the slope's steepness alone.
Key takeaways
  • Simple linear regression finds ŷ = b₀ + b₁x by minimizing the sum of squared residuals.
  • R² tells you the share of variance in y explained by x — not whether the model is "correct."
  • Never extrapolate confidently outside the range of your observed x values.

MODULE 3 · APPLICATION

Multiple Regression & Model Evaluation

Real outcomes rarely have just one cause. Now we add more predictors — and learn how to tell whether the model is actually trustworthy.

Adding more predictors

Multiple linear regression extends the same idea to several predictors at once:

ŷ = b₀ + b₁x₁ + b₂x₂ + b₃x₃ + ... + bₙxₙ

Each coefficient bᵢ now means "the expected change in y for a one-unit increase in xᵢ, holding all other predictors constant." That last clause matters enormously — it's what separates multiple regression from just running several simple regressions separately.

Analogy Think of predicting how good a loaf of bread turns out. Flour amount, water ratio, yeast, kneading time, and oven temperature all influence the result simultaneously. Multiple regression is like a recipe that quantifies each ingredient's individual contribution to the final loaf — while holding the others fixed — rather than testing one ingredient at a time in isolation.

Adjusted R²: penalizing complexity

Plain R² can only go up (or stay flat) as you add more predictors — even useless, random ones. Adjusted R² corrects for this by penalizing the model for extra predictors that don't pull their weight:

Adjusted R² = 1 − [(1 − R²)(n − 1) / (n − k − 1)]  (n = observations, k = predictors)

If adding a variable barely improves R² but adjusted R² actually drops, that variable is likely just noise.

The LINE assumptions

AssumptionWhat it means
LinearityThe true relationship between predictors and outcome is a straight line (or transformed to be one)
IndependenceResiduals aren't correlated with each other (no hidden time or grouping structure)
NormalityResiduals are roughly normally distributed around zero
Equal varianceResidual spread stays constant across all predicted values (homoscedasticity)

Reading a residual plot

Plotting residuals against predicted values is the fastest way to check the LINE assumptions visually.
Practical exercise

Select a scenario and diagnose which assumption is being violated.

Common misconceptions
  • "More predictors always make a model better." Extra variables can overfit — the model memorizes noise in your sample instead of learning the true pattern, so it performs worse on new data.
  • "Two highly correlated predictors just add extra confidence." That's multicollinearity, and it actually makes individual coefficient estimates unstable and hard to interpret.
  • "A high R² alone proves the model is valid." A model can have R² = 0.95 and still badly violate the LINE assumptions.
Key takeaways
  • Multiple regression coefficients are "holding everything else constant" effects, not standalone ones.
  • Adjusted R² penalizes useless predictors; plain R² never decreases when you add variables.
  • Always check LINE assumptions with residual plots before trusting a regression's conclusions.

MODULE 4 · MASTERY

Practical Application & Mastery

Here's where textbook regression meets messy real data: non-linear patterns, outliers, deceptive aggregates, and how experienced analysts protect themselves from being fooled.

When the relationship isn't a straight line

Not every real relationship is linear. Growth, decay, and saturation effects often follow curves. Two common fixes:

  • Log transformation: compresses large values, useful for exponential-looking growth (income, population, pricing)
  • Polynomial terms: adding x² lets the line bend, useful for U-shaped or peak-then-decline patterns

Anscombe's Quartet: the same statistics, four different truths

All four datasets share nearly identical r, R², slope, and intercept — yet tell completely different stories. Statistics summarize; they don't replace looking at the plot.
Analogy A regression model is like a compass: extremely useful for finding direction, but only as trustworthy as the assumptions it was built on. Hand a compass to someone standing next to a large magnet (an outlier, a confounder, a broken assumption) and it will confidently point you the wrong way.

Simpson's Paradox

A trend that appears in several groups of data can reverse — or disappear — when those groups are combined. A treatment might look worse overall while actually performing better within every single patient subgroup, if the subgroups differ in size and severity. This is exactly why a correlation matrix on aggregated data can mislead without checking for hidden subgroups.

Predictive modelingCausal inference
Goal: forecast y accurately from xGoal: determine whether changing x changes y
Correlational data is often enoughUsually needs randomization or careful confound control
A confounder can still help predictionsA confounder invalidates the causal claim
Example: predicting exam scores from study hoursExample: proving that studying more causes higher scores
Practical exercise · mini case study

A city-wide dataset shows a strong negative correlation (r = -0.6) between number of fire trucks sent to a fire and the amount of damage. Which conclusion is correct?

Common misconceptions
  • "A high R² always means a good, deployable model." It can still be overfit, violate assumptions, or rely on a variable you won't have at prediction time.
  • "Removing outliers is always the right move." Sometimes the outlier is the most important data point — investigate before deleting.
  • "Aggregated trends always reflect subgroup trends." Simpson's Paradox proves this can flip entirely.
Key takeaways
  • Identical summary statistics (r, R², slope) can describe wildly different underlying data — always visualize.
  • Predictive power and causal validity are different goals that require different evidence.
  • Check for confounders and subgroup reversals (Simpson's Paradox) before trusting an aggregate trend.

Summary Notes

  1. Correlation (r) quantifies the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables, from -1 to +1. It never proves causation on its own.
  2. Simple linear regression fits ŷ = b₀ + b₁x by minimizing the sum of squared residuals — the least-squares method.
  3. R² reports the proportion of variance in y explained by the model; in simple regression, R² = r².
  4. Multiple regression extends this to several predictors, with each coefficient interpreted holding the others constant; use adjusted R² to penalize useless predictors.
  5. The LINE assumptions (Linearity, Independence, Normality, Equal variance) should be checked with residual plots before trusting any regression output.
  6. Anscombe's Quartet and Simpson's Paradox are permanent reminders that identical summary statistics can hide very different, even contradictory, underlying realities — always visualize your data.
