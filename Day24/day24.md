## 5. Key Learnings

- **A good product plan isn't a business plan.** Day 23 nailed the MVP scope, but this report exposed that pricing and distribution — the actual mechanics of becoming a business — were still just assumptions, not facts.
- **"Who pays" and "why they pay" are different questions worth separating.** Knowing the budget-holder (Ops lead) doesn't automatically mean I know the reason they'd actually sign — recurring time cost, not feature excitement, is the real hook.
- **A moat you haven't earned yet is just a hope.** Listing "data moat" and "workflow lock-in" felt reassuring until the report flagged that none of them exist without a shipped product and real usage data — that reframing was more useful than the moat list itself.
- **Reverse SWOT surfaced a bias I hadn't noticed.** Deep founder domain expertise is listed as a strength everywhere, but flipping it exposed a real risk: building for my own past pain instead of a validated broader market.
- **The scorecard being uneven (35 on Investor Readiness vs. 55 on Viability) was more useful than one blended number.** It pinpoints exactly where the gap is — not "the whole idea is weak," but specifically "there's no proof yet, and that's fixable in 60–90 days."
- **A visual dashboard forces prioritization.** Fitting the whole business model, GTM flow, and scores onto a single infographic meant cutting anything that wasn't essential — a good discipline check on the report itself.
